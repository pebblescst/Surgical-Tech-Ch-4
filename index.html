Why this matters: The surgical technologist has legal and ethical obligations in every case. Understanding medicolegal concepts protects the patient, protects you, and is frequently tested on the CST exam.
Law vs. Standard vs. Code of Conduct
Law
Rules enacted by government (federal, state, administrative); legally binding; violation results in criminal or civil penalties (fines, loss of license, imprisonment)
Standard of practice
Level of care a reasonably competent professional would provide under similar circumstances; used in court to determine negligence; set by professional organizations (AST, AORN) and The Joint Commission
Code of conduct
Organization's rules for member behavior (e.g., AST Code of Ethics); NOT a law — no legal consequence for violation, but professional consequences apply (censure, expulsion, loss of certification)
Licensure vs. Certification vs. Registration
Licensure
Required in all states to practice for licensed professions (RN, MD, PA). Government-issued right to practice. Cannot practice without it in states where required. Some states now require ST licensure.
Certification
May or may not be required depending on employer/state. Awarded by a professional organization (NBSTSA → CST) upon passing an exam. Demonstrates voluntary attainment of a recognized competency standard.
Registration
The state maintains an official record of qualified practitioners. Less rigorous than licensure; may simply require meeting criteria and paying a fee to be listed on a state registry.
Key distinction: Licensure = required by law to practice. Certification = credential demonstrating competency (may or may not be required). Registration = state record-keeping.
Types of Law
Federal law
Enacted by the U.S. Congress; applies to all states; supersedes conflicting state laws. Examples: HIPAA, OSHA regulations, ADA, anti-discrimination statutes
State law
Enacted by state legislatures; governs healthcare practice at the state level; includes nurse practice acts, ST licensure laws, and scope of practice regulations
Administrative law & regulations
Rules created by government agencies (OSHA, FDA, CMS); have the force of law; govern specific areas like workplace safety, drug approval, and Medicare/Medicaid compliance
Civil law
Governs disputes between private parties; includes tort law (negligence, malpractice); remedy is financial compensation (damages), not imprisonment
Criminal law
Governs offenses against society/the state; violations can result in fines, probation, or imprisonment. Examples: assault, theft, Medicare fraud, practicing without a license
Common law
Law derived from court decisions and legal precedents (case law); evolves over time; used when no statutory law directly applies; forms the basis of negligence standards
Corporate law
Governs the legal structure and liability of corporations (hospitals, health systems); addresses institutional responsibility, board governance, and corporate negligence (hospital liable for system failures)
Risk Management
Primary goal: Prevent harm to patients, staff, and visitors through proactive identification and mitigation of risks.
How risk is managed:
✦ Education — keep staff trained on policies and safety protocols
✦ Promotion of safety measures — culture of safety; speak-up environment
✦ Compliance with safety measures — follow established protocols every time
✦ Follow-up — track incidents; evaluate effectiveness of interventions
✦ Staff training — regular competency assessments and in-service education
✦ Reporting policies — standardized incident reporting systems
✦ Cooperation with risk managers — transparent, timely reporting of events
Professional Standards Organizations
American Academy of Surgeons
Sets surgical standards and best practices for the surgical profession
AST — Association of Surgical Technologists
Sets standards of practice, code of ethics, and position statements for surgical technologists
AORN — Association of Perioperative Registered Nurses
Publishes evidence-based perioperative nursing standards and recommended practices
Evidence-based practice: Clinical decisions guided by the best available research evidence, clinical expertise, and patient preferences. Position statement: An official stance published by a professional organization on a specific issue.
OR procedure manual: Facility-specific document describing protocols for specific OR practices; all staff are expected to follow it. Violation may result in disciplinary action.
Torts — Legal Wrongs Causing Harm
Negligence tort
Failure to use such care as a reasonably prudent and careful person would use under similar circumstances. Unintentional; does not require intent to harm. Most common tort in healthcare.
Intentional tort
Intentional wrongdoing that results in injury or harm to another person. Requires intent. Examples: assault, battery (unauthorized touching/surgery), false imprisonment, defamation.
Medical malpractice
Improper or unethical conduct OR unreasonable lack of skill by a holder of a professional position. A form of negligence specific to licensed healthcare professionals; requires: duty, breach, causation, damages.
Negligence requires proof of 4 elements: (1) Duty — the professional owed a duty of care to the patient; (2) Breach — they failed to meet the standard of care; (3) Causation — the breach directly caused the harm; (4) Damages — the patient suffered actual harm. All four must be proven.
Common Acts of Negligence in the OR
🧲
Unintended retained foreign objects (URFO)
Sponges, needles, instruments left inside patient after closure; prevented by accurate counts (sponge, sharp, instrument); most serious and common OR negligence claim
🔥
Burns
From electrosurgical units, prep solutions, laser, heated irrigation; prevented by proper grounding, equipment checks, and fire safety protocol
⬇️
Falls
Patient falls from OR table or stretcher; prevented by using safety straps, side rails, and proper assistance during transfers
🛏️
Incorrect patient positioning
Nerve injury, pressure injuries, compartment syndrome from improper positioning; ST must know correct positioning for each procedure type
⚠️
Wrong patient / Wrong site surgery
Performing surgery on wrong patient or wrong body site; prevented by the surgical time-out and site marking protocols; a sentinel event
🔬
Specimen identification / loss
Incorrect labeling, loss, or mishandling of surgical specimens; ST is responsible for proper specimen handling and chain of custody
💊
Medication errors
Wrong drug, dose, route, or patient; the ST must clearly label all medications on the sterile field and confirm with the circulator using closed-loop communication
🚶
Abandonment
Leaving the patient without adequate care or supervision; the scrub tech must never leave the sterile field or patient unattended during an operation
📢
Failure to communicate / miscommunication
Incomplete handoffs, missed critical information, unclear verbal orders; a leading root cause of sentinel events; prevented by SBAR, time-out, and closed-loop communication
👜
Loss / damage to patient's property
Jewelry, dentures, valuables; must be documented and secured before surgery; patient property is the team's legal responsibility while in care
📋
Negligent delegation
Assigning a task to someone who lacks the competency to perform it safely; supervisors are legally liable if they delegate inappropriately and patient harm results
Sentinel event (TJC definition): A patient safety event resulting in death, permanent harm, or severe temporary harm requiring intervention to sustain life. Also called an adverse event. The ECRI Institute is the leading authority on patient safety — www.ecri.org
Common Types of Perioperative Documentation
Patient medical record
Complete health history, physical, orders, and care documentation; legal record of the patient's entire encounter
Informed consent
Legal document signed before surgery; patient agreement after receiving full disclosure of procedure, risks, benefits, alternatives
Intraoperative record
Documents all events during surgery: personnel, times, counts, implants, medications, irrigations, positioning, specimens
Anesthesia record
Real-time record of anesthetic agents, vital signs, airway management, and patient response kept by the anesthesia provider
Patient charges
Itemized list of all supplies, implants, and services used during the procedure; basis for billing
Birth and death certificates
Legal vital records created during obstetric or post-mortem cases in the OR
Specimen and pathology records
Chain of custody documentation for all tissue samples removed during surgery; labels, container, preservative, and disposition
Advance health care directive
Patient's pre-stated wishes regarding medical treatment (DNR, organ donation, power of attorney); must be in the chart before surgery
Documentation is required in all areas of medical practice. The Joint Commission sets documentation standards. All medical records are legal documents.
Guidelines for Documentation
✓
Every document must include the patient's unique identifiers (name, DOB, MRN)
✓
The date must be accurate; never backdate or pre-date entries
✓
Correct errors by drawing a single line through the error, writing the correction, and initialing — never use correction fluid (white-out)
✓
Documentation must be legible in black ink (not pencil, not blue ink in many facilities)
✓
Use brief, exact wording — state facts; avoid opinions and assumptions
✓
Use correct language and spelling; avoid non-approved abbreviations (TJC prohibited list)
✓
Always log off computers; never share passwords — EHR access is audited
✓
Documentation must include your full signature and credentials
Incident / Sentinel Event Report — "Who, Where, When, How": Must include all four. Write and submit ASAP after the event. State facts only — no opinions. Use professional language and first person. Submit to OR director. Informal investigation or insurance notification may follow.
Informed Consent
Informed consent is a legal document signed by the patient before surgery. It represents the patient's voluntary agreement to undergo a procedure after receiving full disclosure.
Five required elements of informed consent:
1
Description of the procedure
What will be done; the name of the surgery, body area involved, and basic surgical approach explained in plain language
2
Risks, benefits, and outcomes
Known risks of the procedure; expected benefits; potential outcomes including success rates and common complications
3
Alternatives to the procedure
Other treatment options available, including non-surgical options; patient must understand they have a choice
4
Patient understanding
The patient demonstrates comprehension of the information provided; must have opportunity to ask questions; interpreter required if language barrier exists
5
Acceptance of the procedure
Voluntary agreement; patient signs; free from coercion; patient may withdraw consent at any time before surgery begins
The surgeon obtains informed consent — not the ST. The ST's role is to confirm consent is present and signed in the chart before the case begins. If consent is missing, notify the circulator immediately — surgery must not proceed without it.
Advance Health Care Directives
Documents that record a patient's wishes regarding medical treatment in advance, typically when the patient may be unable to speak for themselves.
DNR — Do Not Resuscitate
Patient's directive to withhold CPR and resuscitative measures. Must be clearly documented in the chart. The OR team must know if a DNR is in place — standard practice suspends DNR during surgery but this must be discussed with the patient pre-op.
Organ donation
Patient's consent to donate organs/tissues after death. May be documented on driver's license, in a registry, or as part of the advance directive. Triggers specific OR protocols upon declaration of death.
Refusal of blood / tissue products
Patient's directive to decline blood transfusions or tissue products (common among Jehovah's Witnesses). Must be documented and respected; surgical team must use bloodless techniques if applicable.
Medical power of attorney (MPOA)
Legal designation of a person to make healthcare decisions on the patient's behalf when the patient is incapacitated. The MPOA agent can give or refuse consent for surgical procedures.
HIPAA — Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (1996)
HIPAA Privacy Rule: Protects patients' individually identifiable health information (PHI — Protected Health Information). Violations carry significant civil and criminal penalties.
Privacy Rule identifiers that MAY NOT be shared or disclosed without patient authorization:
Name and address
Date of birth
Social Security number
Photographs
Medical record numbers
Fax and phone numbers
Email addresses
Health plan beneficiary numbers
Account numbers
Certificate / license numbers
Vehicle identifiers / license plates
Geographic data (ZIP code, address)
In the OR: Do not discuss patient information in hallways, waiting rooms, or elevators. Do not share cases on social media — even without names. All EHR access is audited. Texting patient information is a HIPAA violation unless an encrypted platform is used.
Developing an Incident / Sentinel Event Report
Must include all four "W"s + "H":
WHO
All individuals involved in the event
WHERE
The exact place / location of the incident
WHEN
Date and time of the incident
HOW
The manner / sequence of events leading to the incident
Report writing guidelines:
✦ Write and submit ASAP after the event
✦ State facts only — no opinions or speculation
✦ Use professional language; avoid emotional language
✦ Write in first person ("I observed...")
✦ Do not be intimidated — you are protected when reporting in good faith
✦ Take your time; be accurate and thorough
✦ Submit to the OR director
✦ Informal investigation or hospital insurance notification may follow
Negligence — Liability & Damages
Liability insurance
Professional (malpractice) liability insurance covers the cost of legal defense and damages if a practitioner is found negligent; some states/employers require it; STs may carry individual policies
Damages
Monetary compensation awarded to an injured party in a civil negligence case. Types: Compensatory (actual losses — medical bills, lost wages, pain); Punitive (punishment for egregious conduct beyond negligence)
Respondeat superior ("let the master answer"): The employer (hospital/facility) is legally responsible for the negligent acts of its employees committed in the scope of their employment. However, the individual practitioner can also be held personally liable.
Workplace Abuse & Disruptive Behavior
Types of workplace abuse:
Bullying
Repeated aggressive behavior intended to intimidate, dominate, or humiliate a coworker; includes verbal abuse, exclusion, sabotage, and intimidation tactics; creates hostile work environment
Hazing
Rituals or practices that involve putting new employees or students through demeaning, uncomfortable, or dangerous experiences as initiation; illegal in many states; prohibited by most healthcare institutions
Sexual harassment
Unwanted sexual advances, comments, or conduct that creates a hostile work environment or affects employment conditions; federally prohibited by Title VII; must be reported through HR/chain of command
Disruptive behavior
Any behavior that disrupts OR function, intimidates staff, or compromises patient safety; includes "surgeon rage," verbal outbursts, throwing instruments, demeaning comments
Effects of workplace abuse:
✦ Decreased staff morale and teamwork
✦ Higher staff turnover and absenteeism
✦ Increased medical errors and patient harm
✦ Psychological trauma to targeted individuals
✦ Culture of fear that suppresses safety reporting
Prevention: Zero-tolerance policies; anonymous reporting systems; education; strong leadership modeling of professional behavior; TJC standards require hospitals to have a process for managing disruptive behavior.
Roots of Disruptive & Violent Behavior in the OR
Traditional hierarchy
Historical "captain of the ship" model placed surgeons above all others; created power imbalances that tolerated and even normalized abusive behavior from physicians toward OR staff
Authoritarian practices
Management or leadership styles that discourage questioning or pushback; suppress the speak-up culture essential for patient safety; reinforce hierarchical silence
Normalization of abuse
When verbal abuse, instrument throwing, or demeaning behavior becomes accepted as "just how things are done," it normalizes harmful behavior and prevents staff from reporting it
The ST's responsibility: Know your rights. Document incidents. Report through proper channels. Patient safety depends on a culture where every team member — regardless of role — can speak up without fear. Disruptive behavior that compromises patient safety must be reported.
Facility standards: Hospitals accredited by TJC must maintain standards and policies and provide orientation training for new employees. Violation of hospital policy may result in disciplinary action up to and including termination. The OR procedure manual describes protocols for specific practices.
